What is a Heatwave?

Understanding the scientific definition and characteristics

Scientific Definition

A heatwave is a period of abnormally hot weather, typically lasting several days or weeks, with temperatures significantly above the long-term average for a region. The World Meteorological Organization defines a heatwave as five or more consecutive days with daily maximum temperatures 5°C above the average maximum temperature.

Regional Characteristics

Heatwaves are closely related to geography. Regions under subtropical high pressure (Mediterranean, Southwestern US) are more prone to heatwaves. Urban areas experience more significant impacts due to the "urban heat island" effect. High humidity in coastal areas increases the perceived temperature during heatwaves.

Health Risks

Heatwaves pose serious threats to human health, causing heat cramps, heat exhaustion, and heatstroke. The elderly, children, people with chronic illnesses, and outdoor workers are particularly vulnerable. The 2003 European heatwave caused over 70,000 deaths, highlighting the lethality of extreme heat.

Causes of Heatwaves

Factors contributing to extreme heat events

Global Warming and Heatwave Frequency

Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Human-produced CO2 and other greenhouse gases cause global temperature rise, increasing heatwave frequency and intensity.

Atmospheric Circulation

Stalled high-pressure systems cause hot air accumulation, with sinking air suppressing cloud formation.

Urban Heat Island Effect

Concrete and asphalt in cities absorb and store heat, making urban areas 3-5°C hotter than surrounding rural areas.

Reduced Vegetation

Deforestation and land degradation reduce evaporative cooling, further increasing surface temperatures.

Impacts of Heatwaves

Socioeconomic and environmental consequences

Global Heatwave Risk Map

Areas in red show highest heatwave risk

Health Impacts

  • Increased risk of heatstroke and exhaustion
  • Worsening of cardiovascular and respiratory diseases
  • Significantly higher mortality among elderly
  • Increased mental health issues
  • Expanded range of infectious diseases

Environmental Impacts

  • Exacerbated droughts and wildfires
  • Accelerated glacial melt and sea level rise
  • Water shortages and quality deterioration
  • Biodiversity loss and species migration
  • Crop failures and food security threats

Socioeconomic Impacts

  • Reduced labor productivity
  • Surges in energy demand stressing power grids
  • Infrastructure damage (roads, rails)
  • Losses in tourism and agriculture
  • Increased burden on healthcare systems

Major Historical Heatwaves

Significant heat events since the 20th century

1936 - North America

The US Midwest and Canada experienced their deadliest heatwave with temperatures reaching 49°C, lasting nearly two weeks and causing over 5,000 deaths.

2003 - Europe

Europe experienced its most severe recorded heatwave, with France reaching 44.1°C, resulting in over 70,000 deaths.

2010 - Russia

Moscow experienced its worst heatwave in 130 years, reaching 38.2°C, causing approximately 56,000 deaths and massive wildfires.

2019 - Europe

Western European countries broke temperature records: France 46°C, Germany 42.6°C, Netherlands 40.7°C, resulting in 2,500+ deaths.

2021 - North America

British Columbia, Canada recorded 49.6°C - 15°C above normal - causing over 600 sudden deaths.

2022 - China

China experienced its strongest heatwave since 1961, with 75 weather stations breaking records, causing severe drought in the Yangtze basin.

Prevention & Response

Strategies at individual, community and government levels

Personal Protection

Stay hydrated, wear loose light clothing, avoid outdoor activities during peak heat, use cooling systems, monitor weather alerts.

Community Action

Establish heat alert systems, create cooling centers, check on vulnerable populations, organize community support networks.

Urban Cooling

Increase green spaces, use reflective building materials, implement green roofs and walls, add water features.

Long-term Strategies

Reduce greenhouse emissions, improve building standards, enhance heat-health research, develop emergency response plans.
